Learning to Work

I was born and raised in Joplin, Missouri, a small, Midwestern town known best for two things: for being the last place Mickey Mantle played minor league baseball before being called up to play for the Yankees, and for the catastrophic, F-5 tornado that ripped through the city and is considered the most devastating in American history.

I wasn't around in 1950 to watch Mantle win MVP for the Miners, but I did live through the tornado. I spent that night first huddled in a pitch-black bathroom, watching leaves wander through the crack in the door (though that door should have led to a hallway), then wandering through the wreckage of our neighborhood to see if the people I had grown up with were still alive.

That night, I learned about terror.

In the aftermath, though, throughout the following summer and years later, I learned a more important lesson - a lesson about work. I learned just how much I was capable of from hauling trees, re-tiling floors, and stripping copper wires on days that reached 105 degrees. I learned the value of good work, of the tired-but-satisfied feeling you only get when you know you've done everything you can do.

From the men and women who flocked into the city to help me, my family, and my neighbors, I learned the value of a good worker, who doesn't need an invitation to bring his own chainsaw to chop up those fallen trees so someone like me could haul them, or to bring cold water to those people on 105-degree days.

Ever since then, I've prided myself on being a good worker, on making good work. Because if I can survive a tornado, and all of the work needed to rebuild a city that came after, then I can certainly stay a little late, arrive a little early.

I'll even bring the chainsaw.
